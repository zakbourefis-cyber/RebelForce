AI Talent Gap: Building Cross-Functional Teams

May 17, 2026

Many businesses struggle with adopting AI due to a shortage of skilled talent. The demand for AI professionals is growing 2–4 times faster than the supply, and hiring for these roles is slow and expensive. But the problem isn’t just technical skills - companies also lack individuals who can connect AI outputs to business goals.

The solution? Build cross-functional teams that combine technical expertise with business knowledge. Instead of searching for a single "AI expert", focus on assembling diverse roles like AI Product Managers, ML Engineers, and Risk Leads. Align these teams with clear business objectives, prioritize impactful AI use cases, and use tools like skills gap analyses to identify internal talent and build AI skills.

Key takeaways:

  • AI Use Cases: Focus on areas like cost savings or improving customer satisfaction by at least 10%.

  • Team Roles: Combine technical experts with leaders who can interpret AI results and guide decisions.

  • Training: Equip employees with role-specific AI skills through structured learning paths.

  • Governance: Strong leadership roles and clear mandates prevent project delays and confusion.

Aligning AI Skills With Business Goals

It all starts with a simple question: where can AI make a real difference for your business? A structured approach is key - connecting your business objectives with AI use cases and the skills required to execute them. This alignment ensures your AI projects are supported by the right expertise, making team-building much more effective. This often requires building a data-first culture to support technical shifts.

Identifying AI Use Cases That Create Value

Take a close look at your business operations. Identify 10–15 processes where AI could deliver measurable improvements - whether it’s cutting costs, saving time, or boosting customer satisfaction by at least 10%. For businesses in the U.S. Virgin Islands, this might involve streamlining tourism bookings, minimizing shipping delays between islands, or automating compliance tasks.

To prioritize effectively, use an impact/feasibility matrix. Score each potential AI use case based on its business impact and the technical effort required to implement it. This approach helps you focus on the most promising opportunities.

Once you've identified and prioritized these use cases, it’s time to define the roles needed to bring them to life.

Defining Role Profiles and Key Competencies

After determining what you want AI to accomplish, the next step is figuring out who needs to make it happen. Instead of searching for a one-size-fits-all "AI expert", align your use cases with specific roles. A helpful framework includes five key profiles: AI Product Manager, Prompt Engineer, ML Engineer, MLOps Engineer, and AI Risk Lead. Each role ties directly to a particular area of value - for instance, an MLOps Engineer might focus on supply chain efficiencies, while a Prompt Engineer could enhance customer support or marketing efforts.

But technical know-how isn’t enough. Every role should also include skills in generative AI literacy, data governance, and risk management. As Allison Bailey, Senior Partner and Global Co-lead of BCG U, explains:

"The AI skills gap doesn't just sit in the workforce. It sits in the C-suite. If leaders don't deeply understand AI, they can't set the right vision or move the organization at the speed this transformation demands."

Leadership roles, in particular, need to focus on "sense-making" - the ability to interpret AI outputs, ask insightful questions, and guide teams through fast-paced changes.

With your roles clearly defined, the next step is to evaluate your current capabilities and identify where the gaps are.

Running an AI-Driven Skills Gap Analysis

Once you’ve outlined the roles you need, it’s time to assess where your organization stands. A skills gap matrix can help. In this matrix, rows represent the roles required, columns represent business functions (like Marketing or Operations), and each cell rates current capabilities on a scale from 0 (none) to 3 (expert). This provides a clear view of your most urgent gaps.

Traditional methods like surveys and annual reviews can be slow, subjective, and tough to scale. AI-driven tools, however, offer a faster, more objective alternative:

Companies leveraging AI-based talent platforms have seen internal mobility rates jump from 21% to 56%. This shows that smarter analysis can unlock the potential of your existing workforce. Instead of rushing to hire, set clear triggers for when new roles are needed. For example, "Hire an MLOps Engineer once three models are in production and manual deployment exceeds two days". This keeps your team lean while ensuring you’re ready to scale when the time is right.

Building Cross-Functional AI Teams

Once you've identified the skills gaps in your organization, the next step is to assemble agile, cross-functional teams. These teams combine expertise from various departments - like operations, marketing, IT, and compliance - ensuring that no single group shoulders the entire responsibility for AI adoption. Research shows that cross-functional collaboration drives both efficiency and creativity in AI initiatives.

Team Structures and Clear Mandates

As organizations evolve, their team structures often shift from centralized models to embedded systems, then to hub-and-spoke frameworks, and eventually to an AI Center of Excellence. Each stage enhances clarity around roles and responsibilities.

One effective way to structure these teams is by splitting them into product squads and enablement squads.

  • Product squads (sometimes called "Applied ML" teams) focus on achieving specific business goals, like improving customer retention or automating routine tasks such as invoice processing.

  • Enablement squads (or "ML Platform" teams) create the tools and infrastructure - like data pipelines, reusable model templates, and monitoring systems - that help product squads work more efficiently.

By dividing responsibilities this way, enablement squads empower product teams to deliver tangible results. To ensure success, set measurable mandates with clear metrics, deadlines, and accountability.

Leadership Roles and Governance Models

Even with a solid team structure, unclear governance can derail AI projects. According to a 2025 MIT Sloan Management Review survey:

"62 percent of enterprise AI initiatives that stalled cited 'unclear governance roles' as a primary or contributing factor - not technology failure, not data quality, not budget constraints."

To prevent this, strong governance must be in place, starting with the right leadership roles. Key positions include:

  • Executive Sponsor: Champions the AI initiative at the executive level and oversees strategic direction and budget.

  • CoE Lead: Manages day-to-day operations and resource allocation.

  • AI Product Owner: Oversees the portfolio of use cases and aligns them with business goals.

  • Risk Lead: Develops the AI risk framework and has the authority to pause projects if safety or regulatory concerns arise.

  • Compliance Lead: Ensures alignment with regulations and oversees audit readiness.

  • Operations Lead: Monitors production systems and handles deployment standards.

In the U.S. Virgin Islands, the Compliance Lead has the added responsibility of navigating both federal and territorial regulations, especially in industries like tourism, finance, and government contracting. This role must translate these requirements into clear criteria for AI deployment.

Additionally, creating an AI Coordination Council can streamline governance. This group, which includes RevOps, department heads, and IT, meets regularly to review new tools, align on metrics, and resolve conflicts when AI outputs from different teams clash. Companies that adopt cross-functional AI governance structures report double the ROI compared to those managing AI on a department-by-department basis.

Using AI to Build Skills and Support Learning

A staggering 93% of leaders and employees acknowledge that underdeveloped skills and insufficient training are holding back their AI progress. Despite this, only 6% of companies have initiated formal upskilling programs. This disconnect is where many AI initiatives falter. Without adequate training, the collaborative strategies discussed earlier lose their effectiveness. Just as cross-functional teams are essential for innovation, focused training equips employees with the skills needed for long-term AI success. These efforts go hand-in-hand with the cross-functional approaches previously outlined.

Building AI Literacy Across the Organization

The aim isn’t to transform every employee into a data scientist. Instead, it’s about ensuring everyone develops enough AI knowledge to improve their work. In the U.S. Virgin Islands, industries like tourism and logistics stand to gain significantly. For example, hotel staff could use AI to summarize guest reviews or craft tailored travel plans, while logistics teams might analyze supply chain data to refine operations.

Start by auditing workflows to identify 3–5 repetitive tasks that can be automated for each team. This method ties training to practical tasks, helping employees see immediate benefits and boosting their confidence.

Creating Role-Specific Learning Paths

Generic training often fails to stick. A tiered approach works better, dividing employees into three categories:

  • AI Beginners: Need basic knowledge and confidence.

  • Tool Users: Require guidance on best practices and workflow integration.

  • Technical Stakeholders: Need advanced skills like prompt engineering, API usage, and data integration.

Roll out a 90-day training plan:

  • Days 1–30: Build foundational AI literacy.

  • Days 31–60: Introduce role-specific workflows.

  • Days 61–90: Develop strategic leadership skills.

Studies show that employees who receive role-specific training are 61% more likely to actively adopt AI tools compared to those who only attend general awareness sessions.

Cross-Functional Learning Methods

One highly effective approach is the AI Champions program. Instead of relying solely on top-down directives, this model identifies team members who are naturally inclined toward AI tools and formalizes their role in sharing expertise. Victor Hoang, Co-Founder & CMO of Rework, explains:

"The fastest way to spread AI adoption across your team isn't scheduling more training sessions. It's finding the two people who already love these tools and giving them a formal role to share what they know."

This strategy works. In fact, peer-nominated AI ambassadors are present in 68% of companies with high AI adoption rates, compared to just 23% in companies with low adoption. A common practice is to assign one champion per 8–12 team members, limiting their commitment to 2–3 hours per week to avoid burnout.

Scaling and Sustaining Cross-Functional AI Teams

Once you've established team structures and clear mandates, the next challenge is maintaining their effectiveness while scaling efforts. Expanding cross-functional AI teams requires thoughtful refinement of workflows and a focus on measurable outcomes. Before diving into automation, it's crucial to evaluate and streamline existing processes.

Simplifying Workflows Before Applying AI

Automating flawed processes only amplifies inefficiencies. While AI can accelerate workflows, it won't fix foundational issues. Start by mapping inter-team workflows and standardizing handoffs. For example, ensure that Marketing consistently provides detailed context to Sales. To pinpoint inefficiencies, track the Handoff Rework Rate - a high rate signals the need to streamline transitions. Standardized inputs and outputs, supported by a shared data model, can ensure all teams work from the same reliable information.

"Departmental AI adoption creates three structural problems: data fragmentation, insight conflicts, and handoff gaps." - Victor Hoang, Co-Founder & CMO, Rework

Tracking Results and Improving Over Time

A Gartner study highlights that organizations focusing solely on tool-level AI metrics miss out on over 70% of the business impact. The real value lies in process-level metrics like cycle time, handoff accuracy, and decision speed. To measure success effectively, consider three categories:

  • Operational: Metrics like cycle time and handoff rework.

  • Financial: Cost per output and revenue cycle improvements.

  • People: Cross-functional adoption rates and team feedback.

To keep efforts aligned, implement a shared data layer - often referred to as an AI Data Spine - that supports all departmental AI tools. Pair this with a monthly AI Coordination Council to monitor metrics, resolve conflicts, and audit cross-functional workflows. Companies with such governance structures are twice as likely to achieve high AI ROI. With these systems in place, you're ready to scale strategically.

Expanding AI Use Cases Across the Organization

Scaling AI initiatives requires a deliberate approach. Using a Hub-and-Spoke model, focus on expanding only those AI use cases that consistently deliver at least a 10% improvement. This ensures that each new AI application aligns with benchmarks established during pilot phases. Avoid chasing trends; instead, prioritize reliability and adoption - key factors that will hold more weight than technical complexity by 2026. Conclusion: Closing the AI Talent Gap Through Teamwork

The AI talent gap is real, but it’s not insurmountable. Tackling this challenge requires more than just big hiring budgets - it calls for a shift in mindset. Organizations that thrive in AI adoption don’t just see it as a technology issue; they treat it as a team design challenge.

The way forward rests on three interconnected actions: aligning AI skills with tangible business goals, forming cross-functional teams with clear responsibilities, and leveraging AI itself to upskill employees across all roles. The stakes are high - 95% of generative AI pilots fail to deliver meaningful business results.

"The differentiation between market leaders and laggards is no longer about who has access to the best models... but who can assemble the high-performance teams capable of integrating these models into secure, reliable, and value-generating workflows." - Nick Moesker, Author

To move from small-scale experiments to enterprise-wide success, organizations need a structured approach. Rebel Force’s four-phase enablement model - Diagnose, Design, Execute, Validate - offers a clear roadmap. This framework transforms isolated pilots into cohesive systems that drive measurable ROI.

Addressing the AI talent gap isn’t a one-off effort; it’s an ongoing process. With AI skills becoming outdated in less than 2.5 years, continuous learning and strong governance are critical. By refining workflows, building resilient team structures, and focusing on measurable outcomes, organizations can ensure their AI initiatives deliver lasting value.

FAQs

What’s the minimum AI team we need to start?

The size of your AI team largely hinges on your company's scale and the complexity of its operations. For startups, a lean team of 3 to 5 members often suffices. This usually includes a full-stack AI engineer and either a product manager or the founder, who can oversee the project. On the other hand, larger companies may need to bring in more specialists to handle specific tasks, ensuring the team can collaborate effectively across different functions and support growth as the business scales.

When should we hire vs upskill internal staff for AI?

Deciding between hiring new talent or training your current team for AI work comes down to what you need most. If your existing team has a solid foundation in relevant skills and you're aiming to build long-term capabilities, investing in upskilling them can be a smart move. On the other hand, if you're dealing with tight deadlines or need advanced expertise that your team can't develop quickly, bringing in external specialists might be the way to go. The key is finding the right balance between meeting immediate demands and fostering sustainable growth for the future.

How do we keep AI governance from slowing delivery?

To keep things moving smoothly, it's important to use governance frameworks that strike a balance between oversight and flexibility. Bringing together cross-functional teams can help speed up decision-making while ensuring everyone knows their role and responsibilities. By using collaborative tools and adaptable frameworks, you can weave governance right into your daily workflows. This not only keeps things transparent but also ensures progress doesn't stall. With this setup, you can roll out AI responsibly while staying aligned with your business objectives.
